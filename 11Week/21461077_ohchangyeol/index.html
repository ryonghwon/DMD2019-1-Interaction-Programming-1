Times New Roman

It is literally the British Times newspaper's dragon font, which was developed in 1932.
It is the most general purpose serif, highly legible and widely used in titles and texts.
I think it's a font that can be used anywhere without any pressure.
Note that a similar font is Times Roman, and the first GODVA logo I introduced
used this Time Roman in the past. The results show that Times Roman and Times
New Roman were actually fonts released at the same time. When the British Times began its general
release of the typeface, two companies, Linotype and Monotype, simultaneously took over the rights to sell,
the former named Times Roman and the latter Times New Roman as the new Romance of Times.

Univers

It's similar to Helvetica, but it's more modern and refined.
It was designed by Adrian Furtiger, a Swiss, in 1957 and announced in early morning
format by Deberny et Pignut, France's largest printing company.
The Font family is also very loyal, and I may personally prefer it to Helvetica.

Myriad

Myriads is an old-style "Surly," born in 1992 with the work of Robert Slimbach,
who designed Adobe Garamond, the British representative Roman font, and Carol Twombly.
At that time, it was produced with the latest font technology called
'multiple master font' and drew high expectations for its usability.
In 2002, Steve Jobs adopted Apple as its official font.
